Case Study 1: The DMS Platform

Reducing Systemic UX Risk in a Legacy Platform

In this project, I focused on identifying and reducing systemic UX risks in a long-lived platform, balancing accessibility, structural stability, and ongoing product demands.

Section 1: Context & Platform Reality

The platform is a decade-old SaaS used daily by Powersports and Motorcycle Dealerships to manage leads, sales, inventory, accounting, and marketing.

Over time, the product expanded feature-by-feature without a unified design or front-end foundation. While the system remained functional, this growth introduced hidden complexity that affected development speed, UX consistency, and long-term scalability.

The challenge was not a failing product, but a mature platform carrying accumulated design and technical decisions, where small changes increasingly created outsized risk.

Section 2: UX Audit - Identifying Systemic Risks

As the platform evolved over more than a decade, UX and front-end Technical Debt accumulated quietly. While functionality remained intact, system-level risks began to surface through inconsistency, fragility, and reduced development efficiency.

UI Inconsistency

The absence of a Design System led to fragmented visual patterns. Similar components were implemented multiple times with slight variations, increasing cognitive load and weakening user trust.

Fragile Front-End Structure

Deeply nested CSS, mixed selector practices, and inline styles made the UI difficult to maintain. Minor changes often caused unintended side effects, slowing iteration and increasing QA effort.

Development Bottlenecks

Developers spent excessive time navigating legacy code instead of delivering improvements. This indirectly impacted UX through delayed features and performance complaints.

Accessibility and Responsiveness Gaps

Inconsistent accessibility and limited mobile support created compliance and usability risks, particularly in regulated markets and non-desktop use cases.

Audit Summary

The platform’s challenges were not isolated UI issues, but compounding systemic risks. Without structural intervention, future changes would become slower, riskier, and more costly.

Section 3: Strategic UX Direction

Following the audit, the core question was not what to redesign, but where to intervene first to reduce risk without stopping product delivery.

Decision 1 - Start with Accessibility and Responsive, Not a Full System Rebuild
I. Context

The platform had accumulated UX and front-end Technical Debt, but ongoing business demands required continuous feature delivery.

II. Options Considered
  • Rebuild the Design System upfront before shipping new features
  • Address high-risk UX gaps while gradually stabilizing the system
III. Decision

I prioritized Accessibility and Responsive improvements as the first intervention.

IV. Rationale
  • Accessibility issues carried immediate legal and compliance risks.
  • Responsive gaps affected real operational use cases, especially quick mobile tasks.
  • Improvements in these areas forced early standardization of layout, structure, and semantic HTML. Laying groundwork for system stability without blocking delivery.
Decision 2 - Stabilize the System Incrementally, Alongside Real Product Work
I. Context

A full Design System and CSS cleanup could not be treated as a standalone project with a fixed end date.

II. Decision

Instead of a big-bang refactor, system improvements were introduced progressively, embedded within ongoing feature development and maintenance work.

III. Rationale
  • This approach reduced risk while keeping the product moving forward.
  • System decisions were validated against real use cases rather than theoretical completeness.
  • Each feature contributed back to system consistency, rather than adding new fragmentation.
Strategic Outcome

This strategy allowed the platform to reduce systemic UX risks while continuing to deliver business value. System stability was not a prerequisite for progress, it was built through it.

Section 4: Accessibility & Responsive as Risk Mitigation

Accessibility and Responsive design were treated not as visual enhancements, but as risk mitigation measures addressing compliance, usability, and long-term maintainability.

Accessibility - Reducing Compliance and Usability Risk
I. Problem Addressed

Inconsistent accessibility practices created legal exposure in regulated markets and limited usability for users relying on assistive technologies.

II. Intervention

Accessibility improvements were implemented to align the platform with WCAG Level AA standards.

  • Applied semantic HTML and ARIA attributes across complex UI flows
  • Improved color contrast, focus states, and keyboard navigation
  • Worked within constraints of legacy and third-party libraries

Rather than aiming for theoretical perfection, efforts focused on meaningful compliance and practical usability.

Responsive Design - Supporting Real-World Usage Patterns
I. Problem Addressed

The platform was primarily designed for desktop, while mobile usage was growing for specific operational tasks.

II. Intervention

Responsive behavior was introduced based on actual usage context.

  • Optimized layouts for non-desktop access without attempting full feature parity
  • For complex data tables constrained by third-party libraries, a Card View with Quick Actions was introduced
  • This provided a “good enough” mobile experience for quick lookups, avoiding unnecessary complexity

This approach balanced usability improvements with development effort and technical limitations.

System-Level Impact

Accessibility and Responsive work acted as an early stabilizing layer for the platform.

  • Encouraged consistent layout and structural patterns
  • Reduced ad-hoc UI decisions
  • Established constraints that later Design System and CSS improvements could build upon
Outcome

By addressing accessibility and responsive gaps early, the platform reduced compliance risk and expanded practical usability, while creating a more stable foundation for ongoing UX and system improvements.

Section 5: System-Level Interventions

Design System & Front-End Structure Stabilization

Rather than treating the Design System as a standalone deliverable, it was introduced as a structural intervention to reduce UX inconsistency and front-end fragility over time.

Establishing a Single Source of Truth
I. Problem Addressed

UI inconsistency and duplicated implementations increased cognitive load for users and maintenance cost for the team.

II. Intervention

A Design System was defined to standardize visual language and interaction patterns across the platform.

  • Defined core color palettes, typography hierarchy, and spacing rules
  • Consolidated recurring UI patterns into reusable components
  • Established clear usage guidelines to prevent future divergence

This created a shared reference point for design and development, reducing ambiguity and repeated decision-making.

Translating Design Consistency into Code Structure
I. Problem Addressed

The existing CSS structure was fragile, tightly coupled, and prone to side effects.

II. Intervention

Design decisions were translated directly into code standards.

  • Introduced SASS variables to centralize visual tokens
  • Standardized naming conventions and reduced deep selector nesting
  • Gradually removed inline styles and duplicated rules during active development

Rather than a full refactor, cleanup occurred incrementally, prioritizing areas touched by ongoing work.

System Stabilization Through Progressive Adoption

The Design System was not enforced all at once. Adoption happened progressively as features were updated or introduced.

This approach ensured:

  • System rules were validated by real use cases
  • Legacy constraints were respected without blocking progress
  • Consistency increased over time instead of regressing with each new feature
Outcome

The platform shifted from ad-hoc UI decisions to a more predictable and maintainable system. Design consistency improved, front-end changes became safer to implement, and future UX work could build on a more stable foundation.

Section 6: Targeted UX Improvements Enabled by System Stability

As systemic risks were reduced through accessibility, responsive, and structural improvements, the platform gained the flexibility to address high-impact user problems without introducing further fragmentation.

These initiatives were intentionally selective, focused on areas where UX improvements could deliver clear operational or strategic value.

Dealer-to-Dealer Inventory Search: Turning Inventory into a Network Asset
I. Problem Addressed

Dealers struggled to meet customer demand when specific units were out of stock, while slow-moving inventory remained unsold across the network.

II. UX & System Considerations
  • Required consistency in core data models across multiple dealers
  • Needed clear trust boundaries to encourage participation
  • Depended on predictable UI patterns to avoid adding complexity
III. Solution

A dealer-to-dealer inventory search was introduced, allowing dealers to discover available units beyond their own inventory.

  • Privacy controls enabled dealers to define what data was shared and at what level
  • Search scope and visibility were clearly communicated to maintain trust
  • Data integrity was enforced by limiting results to official manufacturer units, ensuring consistent identifiers across participants
IV. Outcome

The platform evolved from a single-dealer tool into a connected ecosystem, supporting collaboration without compromising data reliability or user confidence.

Physical Inventory Counting on Mobile: Designing for Context, Not Feature Parity
I. Problem Addressed

Inventory counting in storage areas was cumbersome when using desktops or laptops, leading to inefficient and error-prone workflows.

II. UX & System Considerations
  • Mobile usage was task-specific and time-sensitive
  • Desktop complexity could not be directly transferred to mobile
  • Input accuracy and speed were more critical than full feature access
III. Solution

A mobile-first inventory counting flow was designed as a focused, specialized experience.

  • Optimized for fast numeric input and touch interaction
  • Stripped down to essential actions only
  • Intentionally diverged from the desktop UI to match real-world constraints
IV. Outcome

Users gained a practical tool aligned with their physical work environment, improving efficiency without increasing system complexity.

Why These Improvements Were Possible

These UX improvements were not isolated feature builds. They were enabled by earlier efforts to stabilize layout structure, accessibility patterns, and front-end conventions. By operating within emerging system constraints, each feature reinforced consistency rather than adding new exceptions.

Section 7: Outcomes & Observed Impact

While not all outcomes were captured through quantitative metrics, the changes resulted in clear, observable improvements in how the platform was designed, built, and evolved over time.

Increased System Predictability

Design and front-end decisions became more consistent and easier to reason about. Shared patterns and constraints reduced ambiguity, allowing teams to move forward with greater confidence and fewer unintended side effects.

Safer and Faster Iteration

Incremental changes were less likely to break unrelated areas. QA cycles became more focused, and UX improvements could be introduced with reduced risk compared to earlier stages of the platform.

Reduced Compliance and Operational Risk

Achieving WCAG Level AA alignment addressed accessibility gaps that previously posed legal and usability risks.

Responsive improvements expanded practical usability without introducing unnecessary complexity.

Clearer UX Trade-Offs

UX decisions shifted from reactive fixes to intentional trade-offs. Limitations, such as “good enough” mobile experiences for non-core use cases, were explicitly defined, documented, and aligned with real usage context.

Stronger Foundation for Future Work

Rather than locking the team into rigid solutions, the platform gained a more stable baseline that future features and improvements could build upon incrementally.

Key Takeaways

This project reinforced how UX problems in long-lived platforms rarely exist in isolation. Most issues were symptoms of deeper systemic instability rather than isolated design flaws.

UX as Risk Management

UX work was most effective when framed as risk reduction, not just visual improvement. Addressing accessibility, responsiveness, and structural consistency early helped prevent larger issues from compounding over time.

Systems Over Screens

Improving individual screens had limited value without stabilizing the underlying system. Design Systems and front-end structure became tools to support safer decisions, not just faster delivery.

Progress Over Perfection

Stability was built incrementally, alongside real product demands. Practical constraints and trade-offs were acknowledged rather than avoided, resulting in solutions that could be sustained.

Design as a Bridge

The work sat at the intersection of user needs, technical constraints, and business risk. Bridging these perspectives enabled UX decisions that were realistic, defensible, and aligned with long-term platform health.

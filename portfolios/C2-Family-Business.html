Case Study 2: Modernizing a Family Business

Context & Constraints

This project was conducted in a small, family-owned garden and landscaping supply shop operating with highly traditional, paper-based processes. The business had remained stable for years, but daily operations relied heavily on manual records and on-the-spot judgment rather than accessible data.

Only two front-of-house staff interacted directly with operational systems. Sales staff at the counter, one of whom also acted as the store manager responsible for procurement, attendance tracking, and payroll calculations. The rest of the workforce consisted of labor-based roles with no system interaction, reinforcing the need for a simple and clearly scoped solution.

Several constraints defined the problem space:

  • SME scale with limited budget, ruling out enterprise POS systems or long-term custom development
  • Low technical confidence, among system users, increasing the risk of resistance and errors
  • Non-standard inventory, including items without barcodes or consistent SKUs
  • Business continuity requirements, where daily operations could not be disrupted for transformation

Rather than treating these as limitations to overcome, I approached them as design constraints that shaped every decision by anchoring the solution in practicality, clarity, and long-term usability.

Problem Framing

Initial issues appeared operational such as frequent stock shortages, time-consuming end-of-day summaries, and limited visibility into sales performance unless someone was physically present at the shop.

However, closer observation revealed that these were only surface-level symptoms.

The core problem was not the absence of tools, but the absence of a shared source of truth.

Sales data existed only on paper, making it costly to aggregate and nearly impossible to analyze. Stock decisions relied on visual estimation, while payroll and attendance summaries required manual calculations twice a month. As a result, decisions were delayed, inconsistent, and heavily dependent on individual experience.

From a stakeholder perspective, the pain points differed:

  • Owner: Limited real-time visibility and reliance on intuition for procurement decisions.
  • Front-of-house staff: Repetitive calculations, fear of making irreversible mistakes, and low confidence in digital tools.
  • Business: Missed sales opportunities, inefficient purchasing, and no clear understanding of which products drove cash flow.

Reframing the problem around data trust rather than software implementation, shifted the goal from “digitizing records” to enabling confident, data-informed decisions across daily operations.

Decision & Trade-offs

All key decisions in this project were shaped by the scale of the business and the realities of a traditional SME environment. The goal was not to implement the most advanced system, but the most appropriate one.

Platform choice: Low-code over enterprise or custom build
  • Off-the-shelf POS systems were cost-prohibitive and assumed standardized inventory and high technical readiness.
  • Fully custom development offered flexibility but exceeded the business’s time and maintenance capacity.
  • A low-code approach (Google AppSheet + Google Sheets) was chosen to prioritize affordability, fast iteration, and adaptability.
Speed of iteration over UI perfection

Using a low-code platform limited fine-grained UI control. This trade-off was accepted to enable rapid changes based on real usage, which mattered more than pixel-perfect interfaces in this context.

Tablet-first interaction model
  • Tablets aligned better with existing staff behavior and physical workspace at the counter.
  • The decision minimized behavioral change and reduced anxiety among low-tech users.
Role-based access instead of feature-rich screens
  • Only two front-of-house staff interacted with the system.
  • Interfaces were scoped by role to reduce cognitive load and error risk.
Deferred optimization by design
  • Automation features (alerts, payroll, attendance) were intentionally postponed.
  • Establishing trust in daily data entry was prioritized over early efficiency gains.

Across these trade-offs, decisions consistently favored clarity, stability, and adoption over technical sophistication or theoretical scalability.

Execution & Iteration

Execution was designed as a gradual transition rather than a full system rollout. Adoption, not deployment, was treated as the primary success metric.

Phased rollout to reduce risk
  • Phase 1: Digital Sales Orders only (staff)
  • Phase 2: Purchase Orders, attendance, and payroll automation (manager)

Responsibilities were expanded only after usage became habitual and data reliability was established.

Progressive access over immediate automation
  • PO creation was initially restricted to the owner
  • Access was expanded once confidence and accuracy improved
  • Automation features were deliberately deferred to avoid amplifying early errors
Iteration through observation

Instead of formal training or documentation, iteration was driven by direct observation of daily workflows. Friction points were addressed by simplifying inputs and aligning screen flow with real counter behavior.

Change management through framing

The system was consistently positioned as a tool to reduce repetitive work, not as a monitoring mechanism. This framing played a key role in reducing resistance among low-tech users.

Throughout execution, my role focused on facilitation and decision-making, ensuring that technical complexity never exceeded what the team and business could realistically support.

Outcome & Impact

The impact of the system extended beyond operational efficiency. Its primary value lay in improving decision quality and reducing uncertainty across daily operations.

Improved data reliability and visibility

Sales and stock information became accessible in near real time, enabling remote visibility without requiring physical presence at the shop. Inventory discrepancies were reduced to under 3%, despite the absence of barcodes or standardized SKUs.

Meaningful time savings in routine work

Manual end-of-day sales summaries, which previously took approximately 30 minutes, were significantly reduced. Payroll and attendance calculations once requiring about one hour per cycle were simplified through structured data and basic automation.

Shift from intuition-driven to evidence-informed decisions

Procurement decisions were no longer based on visual estimation alone. Historical sales data provided a clearer understanding of product performance and cash flow contributors, allowing purchasing decisions to be made with greater confidence and consistency.

Increased staff confidence and system trust

As the system matured, staff hesitation toward digital tools diminished. Role-based access and progressive responsibility helped reduce fear of irreversible mistakes, replacing avoidance with routine use and ownership.

Stronger operational governance

Role-based access controls improved data integrity and protected sensitive financial information. Decision-making became less dependent on individuals and more grounded in shared, reliable information.

Overall, the system shifted the business from reactive operations toward a more stable, data-informed mode of management without increasing technical or cognitive burden on the team.

Reflection as a Practitioner

This project reinforced my belief that effective UX is rarely about interfaces alone. In environments with limited resources and low technical confidence, the real challenge lies in designing systems that people trust enough to use consistently.

Working in a constrained SME context, I experienced product ownership not as feature delivery, but as continuous decision-making. Wearing multiple hats as facilitator, decision-maker, and builder, highlighted the importance of sequencing, scope control, and timing over technical ambition.

Rather than pursuing feature completeness or visual refinement, I focused on reducing cognitive load, managing risk, and building confidence through progressive change. Decisions such as limiting access, deferring automation, and simplifying inputs were intentional UX choices, designed to support real behavior, not ideal workflows.

Progress came not from building more features, but from asking the right questions at the right time:
What is safe to change now? What should wait? And what level of accuracy is “good enough” to move forward?

From an optimization perspective, the most valuable outcome was not efficiency, but reliability. Once data became trustworthy, better decisions followed naturally. By grounding decisions in observed behavior and operational realities, I was able to introduce change without disrupting daily work.

This experience shaped how I approach complex systems, prioritizing clarity over sophistication, people over processes, and long-term usability over short-term wins. It also strengthened my interest in roles that sit at the intersection of business, users, and systems, where value is created not by building more, but by deciding better.

Key principles
  • Adoption is a design problem, not a training problem
  • Optimization should follow trust, not precede it
  • Good products emerge from sequencing, not speed
  • The right solution is often the most appropriate one, not the most advanced
